Lawmakers Move Away from Creating New State Water Agency
A signature piece of the bipartisan "Mega Water Bill" currently being considered by the Kansas Legislature would have created a Kansas Department of Water and Environment, by consolidating various existing water programs that are spread across a half dozen state agencies. Environmental groups, municipal water utilities and water experts had lauded the committee's effort to elevate water as a concern in Kansas, allocate money to the issue and clarify a confusing web of governmental departments. But a revised version of House Bill 2686 that was advanced last week by the House Water Committee gutted any meaningful change to Kansas water policy or to the structure of the agencies that are tasked with implementing it. Virtually all that remains of the original 287-page bill is a reporting requirement on Groundwater Management District finances and their proposed conservation actions. Ron Highland, a Wamego Republican, and the committee's ranking Democrat, Rep. Lindsay Vaughn of Overland Park, said they were frustrated by the outcome. "We got railroaded by an agriculture group," Highland told reporters. "Not a lot of major decisions will be made now because their voice will not be at the level I feel it should be." The bill could evolve further when it hits the floor of the Kansas House and continues its flow through the legislative process. Highland was non-committal on if he would seek to re-insert some of the more controversial elements, including the agency reorganization, back into the legislation. Agricultural groups argue it is better to work with farmers to solve the state's water issues, rather than rely on government officials in Topeka for solutions, and raised concerns about increasing government bureaucracy and financial strain on farmers. But as previously noted by Rep. Highland and other legislators from both political parties, GMDs have existed for decades now, with not a lot to show for their efforts. "With the exception of one, not much has been done," Highland said. [source]


Lansing City Council to Submit BASE Grant Proposal
The Lansing City Council last week voted to move forward with a BASE Grant Proposal to fund a sewer line project. BASE, which stands for Building a Stronger Economy, is intended for projects statewide that create a positive economic impact. The state also indicated it would look more favorably on grants submitted jointly by governmental entities. With that in mind, Lansing staff contacted Leavenworth County about a joint grant application, and county commissioners agreed. Vandall said the commissioners committed to funding the project up to $500,000 and also made it their No. 1 priority among the county's grant applications. Funding for the approximately $4.5 million project includes a $2.9 million Kansas BASE grant, $500,000 for Leavenworth County and $1.1 million to the city of Lansing. [source]

Website Ranks Kansas as Least Beautiful State
News and media website Thrillest recently named Kansas the least beautiful state in the country. Other central states including Iowa, Missouri, Nebraska and Oklahoma also ranked near the bottom. To create the flawed list, the publication found a group of writers who have been fortunate enough to see all 50 states and had each writer rank the states. The group compared rankings and landed on the final list after considering each state's natural landscape, according to the article. Now, the Kansas City Star would like to prove the list wrong and is soliciting photos to do so. The Kansas Sampler Foundation's list of the eight wonders of Kansas might be a good place to start. Now That's Rural:  Audrey Rupp, HorseThief Reservoir
HorseThief Reservoir is now the largest body of water in southwest Kansas, consisting of 450 acres of water in 1600 acres of prairie. "Everybody said (the lake) would never fill with water," said Audrey Rupp, HorseThief Reservoir District Manager, due to the fickle nature of western Kansas rainfall. "By spring 2010 it was filled to 50% and by July 2016 it was filled to capacity." The reservoir takes its name from a nearby canyon where the old-timers say horse thieves were hung back in frontier days. In 2018, an estimated 100,000 visitors came to HorseThief Reservoir from many states.  "We have 54 camping sites with utilities and primitive camping sites all over," Audrey said. [source]

Severe Weather Preparedness Week: March 7 to 11
Tornado season typically ramps up in Kansas during April and especially in May and June. To remind Kansans to prepare for the coming severe weather season, Gov. Laura Kelly has issued a proclamation designating March 7-11 as Severe Weather Preparedness Week in Kansas. "Preparedness is the best defense against the severe springtime weather we typically have in Kansas," Kelly said. "Last year, there were no deaths attributed to tornadoes in the state, thanks in part to our early warning systems and the preparedness measures taken by Kansans across the state." Kelly also credited the cooperative information and education efforts of the Kansas Division of Emergency Management, the National Weather Service, county emergency management agencies, and media partners. "We have a great working relationship with other state agencies, private organizations and news outlets," said Angee Morgan, deputy director of the Kansas Division of Emergency Management. "KDEM is committed to keeping Kansans safe and we could not do it without the help of all these partners." As part of the week's activities, Kansans are urged to take part in the statewide tornado safety drill 10 a.m. Tuesday, March 8. This year, to avoid any confusion to the public, a live tornado warning will not be sent out on the NOAA Weather Radio. Instead, there will be a Routine Weekly Test message asking people to practice sheltering. "We are asking county emergency managers to sound their outdoor warning sirens as part of the drill," said Morgan, "and ask for their help to publicize this drill ahead of time and on the actual test date. We also encourage all Kansans to use this week to make sure their home emergency kits are stocked with up-to-date supplies and to review their home emergency plans." [source]

Drought Monitor
On the Plains, high temperatures exceeded 80°F as far north as Kansas last week. According to the latest U.S. Drought Monitor, severe drought (D2) was broadly expanded across Kansas and Nebraska due in part to short-term precipitation deficits, with corresponding increases in other drought categories. The entire state is now depicted in some category of dryness or drought. Dry and windy conditions contributed to wildfire development across Kansas on Saturday and Sunday, the largest of which were located in Reno and Harvey Counties and dubbed the Cottonwood Complex. The Kansas Forest Service deployed two aviation resources for fire suppression, including Tanker 95, and requested assistance through the Wildland Task Forces. The Kansas Department of Emergency Management also asked two UH-60 Black Hawk helicopters from the Kansas Army National Guard to assist with fire suppression in Reno and Harvey counties on Sunday. The helicopters are flown by members of the 1st Battalion, 108th Aviation, and are equipped with collapsible water buckets.
